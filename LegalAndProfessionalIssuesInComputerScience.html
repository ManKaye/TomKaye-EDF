Privacy & Network Technologies

Privacy - the ability of an individual or group to seclude/isolate themselves or information about themselves and thereby reveal themselves selectively.
Privacy (2) - The right to be left alone.
Physical Privacy - the prevention from intrusions into one's physical space or solitude.
Levels of privacy change between location and time period with relation to privacy law changes, etc.
In terms of computer science, freedom from surveillance is part of privacy. Individuals have an ability to keep electronic communications and personal information confidential.
Individuals have different attitudes towards privacy. These differences can be seen throughout culture and these different attitudes influence individual's behaviour on other people and their privacy.
There are three main types of privacy attitudes:
- Privacy Fundamentalists who place high value on privacy.
- Privacy Pragmatists have strong feelings on privacy just like the fundamentalists, however they can be willing to surrender privacy in certain conditions where care is used.
- Privacy Unconcerned have no real concerns about privacy or how their personal information may be used.
These attitudes can change over time for each individual.
Privacy rights are often expressed in legislation as security like in the Human Rights Act, 1998 and the 4th Amendment to the U.S. Constitution.

Informed Consent

When an individual agrees to something, they should be informed. An informed consent can be said to have been given upon a clear appreciation and understanding of the facts, implications, and future consequences of an action. It is the key to the UK Data Protection Act of 1998.


Opt-out vs. Opt-in

Opting-out is where a user explicitly forbids an organization from sharing information with other organizations. In an opting out environment, both parties involved will mostly keep each other on one another's mailing lists.
Opting-in is where a user explicitly gives permission to the organization that they can distribute the information with the selected organization. This act takes information as a commodity away from companies.
Companies and organizations prefer opting out and opting in is preferred by privacy advocates.
The Privacy and Electronic Communications Regulations, 2003
The UK follows the opt-in scheme under its provisions. In essence, it states that senders cannot send marketing messages unless they have the recipient's prior consent or unless the recipient's email address was collected in the course of a sale. The sender sends only mail related to similar services or products and when the address was collected, the recipient was given the opportunity to opt-out, which they did not take.
Two new rules for e-mail marketing arose from this, the sender must not conceal their identity and the sender must provide a valid address for opt-out requests.


Office of the Information Commissioner

CAN SPAM Act of 2003 (US)

This act allowed for spam e-mail to be sent with the requirement that it must state clearly that it is an advertisement and not an important e-mail. Users can also be given the ability to opt-out from future mailings.

Ethics & Information
Ethical Dilemmas

Ethical dilemmas can occur every day and can be good or bad. For example, illegal activity is a negative ethical dilemma and helping an elderly person cross the road is a positive dilemma.


Information in Modern Society

Workplace pressures regarding information in modern society include the authentication of credentials and the monitoring of employees. Social pressures include CCTV, interception of communications and the need for financial transparency.
When working with this information, it is necessary to have a firm understanding on ethical values and the law, and then to recognize the difference between the two conceptual entities. It is also wise to know that laws are easier to define than ethics.
Ethics - the science of duty, or the branch of knowledge that deals with moral obligations in a society.
Ethics is, in a sense, the even distribution of all individuals' morals in a society to form a median moral, taking into account, all the different cultures and political or religious viewpoints of that society.
There are two main approaches to decoding ethics. One is the utilitarian view and one is the deontological view. The utilitarian view handicaps the party that will have less impact on society. If a man has to save either a baby, or a scientist with the cure for cancer, a utilitarian view would govern that the scientist be saved.
A deontological view will govern that the protocol of society determine the outcome of the ethical dilemma in our current society.
In Layman's terms, utilitarianism governs the morality of an action based on the outcome and deontologism governs the morality of an action based on the duties of the individual.


Ethical Decision-making

In practice we might consider any of the following in making an ethical decision: the best consequences, the moral rights, fairness, the common good and setting an example.


Torts

Tort law deals with our obligation to other member's of society and our liability for our actions.
Every person within society has an obligation not to harm other member's of society. The torts involved with this obligation include: negligence, defamation, breach of confidence and trespassing.


Negligence

Negligence is the situation where an individual performs an action unsatisfactorily and then does not provide communication to any other member of society detailing that the action has been performed unsatisfactorily.


Defamation

Defamation includes statements that harm someone else or their business interests. Slander and libel are the two forms of defamation. Slander is spoken defamation.


Breach of Confidence

A breach of confidence is an instance where personal, private or even Government-classified information is given out to the public through some means.


Trespass

Trespassing is a simple tort, preventing an individual from non-consensually entering another individual's or party's owned land or premises.


Managing Workplace Ethics

A manager of a company can intervene with ethical dilemmas on multiple different levels, depending on the severity of the ethical dilemma. A small and insignificant ethical dilemma may demand an informal discussion accompanied with warnings to the culprits. A slightly more critical ethical dilemma may entail formal discussions and disciplinary procedures. Moving on to even more endangering ethical dilemmas, some of the worst scenarios demand legal or other advice along with mediation and in the worst cases, alerting of the police or security services.


Freedom of Expression

Freedom of expression is the right to express one's ideas or opinions freely through speech, writing or any other form of communication. Freedom of the press is part of freedom of expression as well.
Currently, censorship plays a large part in the controversy of freedom of expression.
Censorship is the suppression and regulation of communication, visually or sonically because the material in question is considered immoral, libelous, damaging to state security or otherwise offensive. Censorship can be pre or post-publication of material and can vary on levels of intensity in a country depending on that country's situation in terms of leadership or whether they are in a state of war.

Data Protection

Informational privacy is the ability for individuals to exert control over their personal information versus the need of authorities and organizations to process personal information to meet their goals and/or provide products and services.
Data protection is relevant as it is meant to help protect your interests as a private citizen.
Data protection laws are governed by the content of personal data, data subjects, controllers and processors, data protection principles, data subject rights and the independent regulator.


Data Protection Principles

- Data is processed fairly and lawfully
- It is obtained for one or more specified and lawful purposes
- Data is processed for adequate, relevant and not excessive reasons
- The data is accurate and up-to-date
- The data is not to be kept longer than is necessary
- The data is processed in accordance with the rights of the data subjects
- Appropriate measures are taking to remedy any unlawful reasons for acquiring data
- Personal data shall not be transferred to another country unless that country has an adequate level of protection for said data


The Rights of Data Subjects

The subjects must be told that their data is being acquired, they must inspect the data themselves, prevent the deletion, amendation and processing of the data that needs to be acquired and can sue for any damages caused to that data.
Unlawful acquirement of data can only override hacking laws if the acquirement of the data is in the best interests of the public as a measure of national security.

Security

Security is the degree of protection against a danger or threat. It is needed to protect valuable assets of some sort.
There are three aspects to a hazard where security would need to be involved: a threat, a vulnerability and a control.
There are four types of threats to information security, these are interception, interruption, modification and fabrication.
Interception is where an unauthorized party has gained access to a private asset.
Interruption is where the private asset belonging to a system is lost, unavailable or unusable.
Modification is where an unauthorized party has tampered with a private asset.
Fabrication is where an unauthorized party has created a fabrication of a private asset which replaces said asset.
The goals of security include three main points.
Confidentiality ensures that only authorized parties are allowed access to the private assets.
Integrity ensures that only authorized parties are allowed to modify data.
Availability ensures that all the authorized parties are allowed access to the private assets at all of the appropriate times.
Building a secure system is finding the right balance between these three goals.


Hackers

Hackers are people who seek to exploit vulnerabilities in a computer system or computer network to obtain illegal access to them.
There are two types of hackers, malicious hackers who can use quick-and-dirty hacking tactics or neat and robust, undetected attacks. There are also ethical hackers who provide penetration tests and vulnerability assessments within a contractual agreement or unpaid security consultants. In other words they hack for the public interest, basically hacking to prevent methods that malicious hackers could find and use to abuse a computer system.


Methods of Defense

There are five methods of defense when it comes to security.
Prevention is blocking the attack.
Deterration is making any attack much harder to succeed.
Deflection is making a dummy target a more attractive victim.
Detection is making it aware that someone is actively trying to hack a computer system.
Recovery is recovering from any attacks to a computer system that do succeed.
These methods can be further split up to preemption, where the hacker is simply prevented from attacking. Another is external prevention where the hackers are prevented from attacking before reaching the system. An external deterrence is where an attacker is deterred while trying to enter the system. An internal prevention will prevent attackers after they have gained illegal access to the computer system. Internal deterrence will deter an attacker after they have gained access to a computer system. Faux environments can be used to lead off detected attackers through deflection. Detection is used to detect the intruders after they are in the system. A response will then be issued to deal with the detected attackers.


Encryption

Encryption is the process of transforming information using an algorithm to make it unreadable to anyone except those possessing special knowledge, usually referred to as a key.
There are two main forms of encryption, asymmetric encryption and symmetrical encryption. Asymmetric encryption is a more complex form of encryption that uses two keys; one for encryption and one for decryption. They are called asymmetric keys. Symmetrical encryption is a form of encryption that uses the same cryptographic keys for both decryption and encryption. These keys are called symmetrical keys.
Encryption addresses confidentiality as it ensures that only authorized parties can gain access to private data. It also addresses integrity in the sense that the data cannot be changed by unauthorized parties.
Weak encryption can be worse than no encryption at all in the sense that it gives users an unwarranted sense of protection.


Cryptography's Network Applications

Authentication is the process of proving one's identity to someone else. In the case of information technology, a digital signature is used to confirm to the receiver that the message received was indeed from the right sender.


Trusted Certification Authority (CA)

Transport Layer Security (TLS)

TLS is the general-purpose protocol used for sending encrypted information over the Internet, through HTTP, FTP, etc. It falls between the transport and application layers.
TLS negotiates a stateful connection between client and server where they establish a connection's security before beginning communication.


TCP/IP Vulnerabilities

Snooping is one vulnerability for the model where potential hackers can gain access to your travelling packets.
Spoofing is where one of the packets that a user has sent is intercepted by a hacker and replaced with a forged packet.


Computer Crime vs. Cybercrime

Computer crime is an offense committed through the use of a computer, so some form of sexual offense or embezzlement offense for example.
A cyber crime refers to offenses against a computer or similar device, so hacking and denial of services, etc.
